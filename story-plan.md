# Story Plan: Cozy Mystery — Christie + French

**Combination ID**: mys-cozy-christie-french
**Genre**: Mystery & Thriller
**Subgenre**: Cozy Mystery
**Target Word Count**: 4,200 words (range: 3,900–4,500)
**Reading Time**: ~17 minutes
**Risk Card**: CONSTRAINT OF SPACE — entire story in one contained location

---

## Writing Samples

### Sample 1: Author A — Agatha Christie (Style)

**Source**: Murder on the Orient Express, And Then There Were None, Poirot short stories, Miss Marple series
**Why representative**: Shows Christie's signature techniques — deceptively simple prose, dialogue-driven characterization, social observation as detection method, misdirection through mundane detail, and the clipped precision of her plotting.

**Prose samples**:

From *And Then There Were None*, Chapter 1 opening — Christie's economical scene-setting through character consciousness:
> In the corner of a first-class smoking carriage, Mr. Justice Wargrave, lately retired from the bench, puffed at a cigar and ran an interested eye through the political news in The Times. He laid the paper down and glanced out of the window. They were running now through Somerset. He glanced at his watch—another two hours to go. He went over in his mind all that had appeared in the papers about Soldier Island. There had been its original purchase by an American millionaire who was crazy about yachting—and an account of the luxurious modern house he had built on this little island off the Devon coast. The unfortunate fact that the new third wife of the American millionaire was a bad sailor had led to the subsequent putting up of the house and island for sale.

From *Murder on the Orient Express*, key dialogue — Christie's detective voice and observational method:
> "I am not one to rely upon the expert procedure. It is the psychology I seek, not the fingerprint or the cigarette ash."

> "The body—the cage—is everything of the most respectable—but through the bars, the wild animal looks out."

> "If you confront anyone who has lied with the truth, he will usually admit it—often out of sheer surprise."

> "The impossible could not have happened, therefore the impossible must be possible in spite of appearances."

> "I do not like your face, M. Ratchett."

From a Poirot story — characterization through social class performance:
> "You have been to the Riviera before, Georges?" said Poirot to his valet the following morning. George was an intensely English, rather wooden-faced individual. "Yes, sir. I was here two years ago when I was in the service of Lord Edward Frampton." "And to-day," murmured his master, "you are here with Hercule Poirot."

From Miss Marple characterization — the detective who observes through social ritual:
> Miss Marple wore a black brocade dress, very much pinched in around the waist, with Mechlin lace arranged in a cascade down the front of the bodice, lace mittens, and a black lace cap surmounted the piled-up masses of her snowy hair, while she was knitting something white and soft and fleecy.

**Christie's style DNA for the writer to internalize**:
- Prose is deceptively simple — avoids ornate descriptions, focuses on clarity and conciseness
- Dialogue carries the majority of the novel; minimal internal narration or setting description
- Characters revealed through mannerisms, speech patterns, and social performances
- Misdirection embedded in mundane social detail — what a character wears, how they hold their teacup, who they sit next to
- Social class and its performance always present — what people choose to reveal about themselves
- The detective observes what others dismiss as trivial, constructs psychology from surfaces
- Interviews structured as "tightly controlled set pieces" — each conversation seems self-contained but contradictions accumulate across them
- Physical clues as social artifacts: a monogrammed handkerchief, a particular type of match, a style of pipe cleaner — objects that encode class, nationality, personal habits

### Sample 2: Author B — Tana French (Style)

**Source**: In the Woods (opening narration, prologue, key passages), The Likeness (opening dream)
**Why representative**: Shows French's atmospheric layering, unreliable first-person voice, psychological depth beneath the procedural surface, and her distinctive Irish literary voice.

**Prose samples**:

From *In the Woods*, opening narration — the detective's confession of unreliability:
> What I warn you to remember is that I am a detective. Our relationship with truth is fundamental but cracked, refracting confusingly like fragmented glass. It is the core of our careers, the endgame of every move we make, and we pursue it with strategies painstakingly constructed of lies and concealment and every variation on deception. The truth is the most desirable woman in the world and we are the most jealous lovers, reflexively denying anyone else the slightest glimpse of her. We betray her routinely, spending hours and days stupor-deep in lies, and then turn back to her holding out the lover's ultimate Mobius strip: *But I only did it because I love you so much.*
>
> I have a pretty knack for imagery, especially the cheap, facile kind. Don't let me fool you into seeing us as a bunch of parfit gentil knights galloping off in doublets after Lady Truth on her white palfrey. What we do is crude, crass, and nasty. A girl gives her boyfriend an alibi for the evening when we suspect him of robbing a north-side Centra and stabbing the clerk. I flirt with her at first, telling her I can see why he would want to stay home when he's got her; she is peroxided and greasy, with the flat, stunted features of generations of malnutrition, and privately I am thinking that if I were her boyfriend I would be relieved to trade her even for a hairy cellmate named Razor. Then I tell her we've found marked bills from the till in his classy white tracksuit bottoms, and he's claiming that she went out that evening and gave them to him when she got back.
>
> I do it so convincingly, with such delicate crosshatching of discomfort and compassion at her man's betrayal, that finally her faith in four shared years disintegrates like a sand castle and through tears and snot, while her man sits with my partner in the next interview room saying nothing except "Fuck off, I was home with Jackie," she tells me everything from the time he left the house to the details of his sexual shortcomings. Then I pat her gently on the shoulder and give her a tissue and a cup of tea, and a statement sheet.
>
> This is my job, and you don't go into it—or, if you do, you don't last—without some natural affinity for its priorities and demands. What I am telling you, before you begin my story is this—two things: I crave truth. And I lie.

From *In the Woods*, prologue — atmospheric setting as character:
> The wood is all flicker and murmur and illusion. Its silence is a pointillist conspiracy of a million tiny noises—rustles, flurries, nameless truncated shrieks; its emptiness teems with secret life, scurrying just beyond the corner of your eye.

> Their bodies have the perfect economy of latency; they are streamlined and unselfconscious, pared to light flying machines. White tattoos—lightning bolt, star, A—flash where they cut Band-Aids into shapes and let the sun brown around them.

> It was your basic Irish summer day, irritatingly coy, all sun and skidding clouds and jackknifing breeze.

From *In the Woods*, on the deformation of memory by time:
> My memories of them had rubbed thin with overuse, worn to frail color transparencies flickering on the walls of my mind: Jamie scrambling intent and surefooted up to a high branch, Peter's laugh arcing out of the trompe-l'oeil dazzle of green ahead. Through some slow sea change they had become children out of a haunting storybook, bright myths from a lost civilization; it was hard to believe they had once been real and my friends.

From *The Likeness*, opening dream — atmospheric prose and sensory immersion:
> Some nights, if I'm sleeping on my own, I still dream about Whitethorn House. In the dream it's always spring, cool fine light with a late-afternoon haze. I climb the worn stone steps and knock on the door—that great brass knocker, going black with age and heavy enough to startle you every time—and an old woman with an apron and a deft, uncompromising face lets me in. Then she hangs the big rusted key back on her belt and walks away down the drive, under the falling cherry blossom, and I close the door behind her. The house is always empty. The bedrooms are bare and bright, only my footsteps echoing off the floorboards, circling up through the sun and the dust motes to the high ceilings. Smell of wild hyacinths, drifting through the wide-open windows, and of beeswax polish.

Additional French voice markers — literary wit and image:
> He was like a huge smug albatross waddling around my desk, squawking vacuously and crapping all over my paperwork.

> In some ways grief anonymizes as powerfully as a Greek tragedy mask.

**French's style DNA for the writer to internalize**:
- First-person narration that is literary, self-aware, and unreliable
- Atmospheric prose that makes setting a character — sensory details psychologically loaded, never decorative
- Long, sinuous sentences that layer imagery; shorter sentences for emotional impact
- Irish voice: dry wit, literary allusions deployed with casual precision, dark humor
- Characters defined by what they refuse to remember or acknowledge
- The gap between professional competence and personal disintegration
- Communities that keep secrets collectively — silence as social contract
- French writes like an actor — first person, showing the story through biases, desires, and fears

### Sample 3: Work X — Murder on the Orient Express (Structure)

**Source**: Structural analysis of the novel's closed-world investigation
**Why representative**: The structural innovation of the closed circle where every suspect is guilty of something, the systematic interview cascade, and the two-solution revelation that reframes the entire narrative.

**Key structural elements to internalize**:

1. **The closed circle**: The Orient Express, snowbound in Yugoslavia, traps all suspects together. The containment is literal and inescapable. The investigator cannot leave; the suspects cannot flee. Geography becomes psychology — who sits where, who is seen in whose corridor, who heard what through the partition wall.

2. **The interview cascade**: Poirot interviews each suspect systematically. Each is "a tightly controlled set piece" revealing facts alongside character tensions. Each story seems plausible in isolation, but contradictions accumulate. The reader assembles a picture the individual characters cannot see.

3. **Every suspect is guilty of something**: Christie's genius — literally every suspect participated. But the deeper principle: each person in a closed community carries a secret they will protect. Some connect to the crime; others are tangential but equally guarded. The investigator must determine which lies matter.

4. **Collaborative red herrings**: The stranger in a conductor's uniform, the woman in a scarlet kimono — distractions planted by the conspirators. Misdirection comes FROM the suspects, not from the narrative.

5. **The two-solution revelation**: Poirot offers two solutions — one simple (outside intruder), one complex (everyone did it). The simple one is false but officially accepted. The complex truth transforms the moral frame: what appeared random murder becomes collective justice. This reframing IS the structural payoff.

6. **Physical clues as social artifacts**: A monogrammed handkerchief, a pipe cleaner, a particular type of match — objects encoding class, nationality, habits. Physical evidence reveals the social world, not just solves the puzzle.

7. **Whydunit over whodunit**: Rather than focusing solely on *who*, the novel emphasizes *why*. Hidden motives reframe innocent actions as damning evidence. The revelation transforms the crime from murder into something morally ambiguous.

**Structural adaptation for the cozy mystery**: Build a closed-community investigation where systematic conversations reveal that everyone is hiding something. Revelations cascade — each interview changes what the previous one meant. The ending should offer a reframing that transforms the detective's (and reader's) understanding of the entire situation.

### Sample 4: Work Y — In the Woods (Themes)

**Source**: Thematic analysis of childhood trauma, unreliable memory, and the investigation that destroys the investigator
**Why representative**: The novel's central argument — that investigation can destroy the investigator, that some cases cannot be solved cleanly, that childhood trauma deforms adult perception.

**Key passages on trauma and memory**:

> In ways too dark and crucial to be called metaphorical, I never left that wood.

> I crave truth. And I lie.

> I coped, in the grand tradition of children everywhere, by retreating into my imagination.

Rob chose "never to remember, to push the events so far back in his mind that he could never find them" — either because "his mind is protecting him from something so horrible it would be unbearable to know, or the more likely, that he just doesn't want to know."

**Thematic architecture**:

1. **The investigation that destroys the investigator**: Rob returns to Knocknaree — the site of his childhood trauma — to investigate a new murder. The professional investigation reopens his personal wound. His attempt to solve the current case becomes entangled with his need to solve his own past. He cannot do both.

2. **Unreliable memory**: Rob's repressed memories surface unbidden during the investigation, but they are fragmentary, untrustworthy, and possibly self-protective fictions. The detective — whose job requires truth — cannot trust his own mind.

3. **The case that cannot be solved cleanly**: The current murder is solved, but the childhood disappearances — the real mystery, the one that matters to Rob — remain unsolved. The novel refuses closure on the wound that drives the narrative.

4. **Childhood trauma deforming adult perception**: Rob functions competently until the case forces him back to the site of his trauma. Then professional self and wounded child-self interfere with each other. Judgment deteriorates. Relationships fracture.

5. **Community silence**: Knocknaree keeps its secrets. The neighbors, the families have absorbed the trauma and closed around it. Silence is not conspiracy but survival — they built lives around not knowing.

6. **The scar trope**: "Scar tissue lacks the flexibility and elasticity of normal healthy tissue." Rob's childhood wound healed into something rigid and brittle. The new investigation applies pressure until it breaks.

**Thematic adaptation for the cozy mystery**: The protagonist investigates a current mystery that inadvertently reopens their own unresolved past. The contained location should be a place the protagonist has history with. The investigation should cost the protagonist something personal. At least one thread should remain unresolved — the wound transforms but does not heal.

## Story Design

### 1. Premise

A retired antiquarian bookseller returns to the rural Yorkshire inn where she spent childhood summers to settle her late aunt's estate, only to find one of the other guests dead in the locked library — and every person sheltering from the late-winter storm has a reason to have wanted the dead woman silenced. As she interviews each guest over the course of a single snowbound night, she discovers that the victim's death connects to a disappearance from the summer she was seventeen — a disappearance Nora herself helped to bury by refusing to remember what she saw.

### 2. Protagonist

**Nora Ballantine**, 62, a retired antiquarian bookseller from Edinburgh who built a quiet, orderly life among old books after a chaotic adolescence. She has returned to Crossbell House — a former country inn in the Yorkshire Dales, now a boutique guesthouse — because her Aunt Philippa, who ran the inn for decades, has died and left Nora the property. Nora hasn't set foot in Crossbell House since the summer she was seventeen, when something happened that sent her to Scotland and kept her away for forty-five years.

- **WANT**: To sell the property quickly and cleanly, closing the chapter on Crossbell House forever. Inventory Philippa's belongings, sign the paperwork, leave within 48 hours.
- **NEED**: To confront what happened the summer she was seventeen — the night her closest friend Judith Lacey disappeared after a party at Crossbell House, and the possibility that Nora's own silence about what she saw contributed to a chain of consequences she has spent four decades refusing to trace.
- **FLAW**: Nora's instinct, when confronted with anything painful, is to catalog and file it — to turn living chaos into organized archives. She treats human behavior as a text to be annotated rather than a reality to be engaged with. She is brilliant at reading other people's evasions and blind to her own. The very skill that made her a superb bookseller (the ability to assess, categorize, shelve) is the skill that allowed her to archive her most frightening memory and never retrieve it.

### 3. Structure

**Opening image/scene**: Nora arrives at Crossbell House in fading afternoon light to find it both heartbreakingly familiar and subtly wrong — the proportions she remembers from childhood have shrunk, the wallpaper has changed, but the smell is the same: woodsmoke and lavender and something underneath, damp stone, the breath of the building itself. Six other people are stranded by a late-winter storm: the estate solicitor, a pair of walkers whose car broke down, a food writer on assignment, the housekeeper who has managed the place since Philippa's decline, and a woman who introduces herself as an old friend of Philippa's. Nora is shown to the library — Philippa's private domain — where she will begin the inventory tomorrow.

**Inciting disruption**: At 10 PM, unable to sleep (the house is too loud with memory), Nora goes to the library to start the inventory early and finds the "old friend of Philippa's" — Sylvie Darnton — dead in the wingback chair. The library door was locked from the inside; Nora entered through the connecting passage from the pantry that only someone who grew up in the house would know. The death looks natural at first (Sylvie is in her late sixties, a glass of whisky beside her), but Nora's trained eye notices details: broken fingernails, a bruise blooming at the wrist, and a page torn from one of Philippa's journals on the desk. The storm has knocked out phone service. No one can leave. No one can call for help until morning.

**Escalating middle**: Nora's bookseller training — her ability to assess, catalog, read between lines — makes her the de facto investigator. She interviews each guest. Each conversation shifts the picture:
- The solicitor (Gerald Farr) has been unusually insistent about the speed of the estate settlement and knows something about the property's debts.
- The walkers (Dennis and Bea Caldwell) are not random strangers — Bea grew up in the village and knew Judith Lacey. They came deliberately, though they are evasive about why.
- The food writer (Rowan Hatch) has been asking questions about the house's history that go beyond restaurant reviews.
- The housekeeper (Mrs. Dillard) has been at Crossbell House twenty years and knows all of Philippa's secrets — or at least the ones Philippa couldn't keep from someone who cleaned her rooms.
- The torn journal page connects Sylvie's visit to the summer of 1981 — the same summer Nora was here, the same summer Judith Lacey vanished.

Each interview is a Christie-style set piece: ostensible subject is Sylvie's death tonight, real subject is the summer of 1981. Every guest is guilty of something — concealment, theft, old betrayals — some connected to Sylvie's death, some not.

**Crisis point**: Mrs. Dillard reveals that Sylvie came not as Philippa's friend but as Judith Lacey's mother — concealing her identity to search Philippa's papers. She was looking for evidence of what happened to Judith in 1981. The torn journal page, which Mrs. Dillard has read, described a confession: Philippa knew what happened to Judith. She saw something from her bedroom window. She never told anyone. And Nora must confront the fact that she, too, saw something that night — something she buried so deep she convinced herself it was a dream — and that her aunt's silence and her own silence were parallel acts of cowardice that left a mother searching for forty-five years.

**Ending**: Nora pieces together that Sylvie died of natural causes — a heart attack, brought on by the shock of reading confirmation of what happened to her daughter in Philippa's journal. The "murder" dissolves into something more devastating: a death caused by truth arriving too late. But the torn page is still missing, and one of the guests took it — someone protecting a living person connected to the events of 1981. Nora must decide whether to find the page or let one more secret stay buried. She chooses to find it. She is done with silence. But the story ends before she reads what is on it. The house settles. The storm continues. One thread stays open.

### 4. Key Scenes

- **The library discovery**: Nora enters through the hidden passage and finds Sylvie. The library must feel alive — its history layered into every surface (books, dust, lamplight on old bindings). Nora's bookseller eye notices what others would miss: the broken fingernails, the bruised wrist, the torn journal. The locked room generates claustrophobic tension. (Advances plot: body discovered. Reveals character: Nora's compulsive cataloging instinct kicks in before grief or fear.)

- **The kitchen interrogation of Mrs. Dillard**: The housekeeper serves tea while answering questions, her hands never still. The domestic choreography of kettle-and-cups becomes a rhythm that controls the conversation's pace. Mrs. Dillard reveals and conceals in the same breath, using hospitality rituals as a screen. Nora recognizes the tactic because it mirrors her own — filing the painful under the mundane. (Advances plot: reveals Sylvie's true identity as Judith's mother. Reveals character: Nora sees her own evasion pattern in someone else.)

- **The hallway confrontation with Bea Caldwell**: Bea breaks down and admits she was Judith's best friend the summer before Nora arrived — the best friend Nora replaced. Bea has carried the guilt of believing she pushed Judith toward recklessness. The hallway is narrow, lit by a single sconce, the physical closeness forcing an intimacy neither woman wants. (Advances plot: fills in the 1981 timeline, reveals Judith was troubled before she vanished. Reveals character: Nora's guilt gains a mirror.)

- **The memory breach**: Alone in the library after the interviews, sitting in Philippa's desk chair (worn to the shape of her aunt's body), Nora allows the suppressed memory to surface. She was at the party. She went outside. She saw a figure near the old stone wall, moving in a way that frightened her. She told herself it was a shadow, a trick of bonfire light. She went back inside. She never mentioned it. The memory arrives not as clean narrative but as fragments: smoke-smell, cold stone under her palm, distant laughter, the wrongness of the figure's movement. (Advances plot: Nora's complicity revealed. Reveals character: the cost of her cataloging instinct — she filed the frightening image like a book to deal with later, and never did.)

- **The final choice**: Dawn light through the library windows. Nora finds the torn journal page pressed inside a book she recognizes — one of Philippa's favorites, shelved in its usual place. Whoever took the page hid it where a bookseller would eventually find it. Nora holds the folded page. She can read it or destroy it. She unfolds it. The story ends. (Advances plot: the mystery of who took the page is implied but unsolved. Reveals character: Nora chooses knowledge over comfort for the first time in forty-five years.)

### 5. Emotional Trajectory

**Opening**: Nostalgic unease — the return to a familiar place that has aged, the gentle dread of going back somewhere you left in a hurry. Warmth in the descriptions of the house, but something clenched underneath.

**Discovery**: Shock sharpened by wrongness — the death is initially ambiguous (natural or foul play?), and the reader's uncertainty mirrors Nora's. The locked library generates claustrophobic pressure. The storm sealing the house tightens the screws.

**Interviews**: Growing fascination and suspicion — each conversation is a small puzzle, and the reader assembles connections. The pleasure is Christie-like: collecting clues, noticing discrepancies, building theories. But underneath, a French-like unease grows — these people are not just suspects; they are wounded, and their wounds connect to a shared past.

**Memory breach**: The floor shifts. What seemed like an external investigation has been, all along, an excavation of the protagonist's own buried complicity. The shift from "who killed Sylvie?" to "what did Nora see and refuse to remember?" should feel both surprising and inevitable. The reader experiences Nora's vertigo.

**Ending**: The weight of a choice being made — not triumphant resolution but the sober courage of someone deciding to face what they have avoided. The open thread (the unread page) leaves the reader sitting with the same anticipatory dread Nora feels. The dominant emotion at the close: *the relief and terror of choosing not to look away anymore.*

### 6. Formula Integration

**Author A — Agatha Christie** (most visible in the **interview cascade**): The sitting-room and kitchen interrogations use Christie's dialogue-driven characterization. Gerald Farr adjusts his cuffs when lying. Mrs. Dillard controls the conversation by controlling the teapot. Dennis Caldwell defers to his wife in a way that is either politeness or concealment. Misdirection comes through mundane social detail — who sits in which chair, who refuses a biscuit, who knows the house layout too well for a first-time visitor. The reader can solve elements of the puzzle from dialogue alone.

**Author B — Tana French** (most visible in the **memory breach** and the **narrative voice**): The first-person narration has French's self-aware, literary quality — Nora observing herself observing, acknowledging her own unreliability. The 1981 memory arrives as sensory fragments: smoke, cold stone, distant laughter, the wrongness of a figure's movement. Crossbell House is rendered as a psychological space — its layout reflecting Nora's interior architecture, the locked library as a metaphor for the compartment of memory she cannot open. Dry wit layered over genuine pain.

**Work X — Murder on the Orient Express** (most visible in the **structural revelation**): The Orient Express innovation was that every suspect was guilty; here, there is no murderer at all — only a cascade of silences that collectively produced a death. Every person in the house is guilty of concealment, and their concealment was a collective act even though none coordinated it. The two-solution structure echoes: the simple answer (natural death) and the complex one (a death caused by forty-five years of community silence). The reframing transforms the story from "who killed Sylvie?" to "who failed Judith, and at what cost?"

**Work Y — In the Woods** (most visible in the **unresolved thread** and Nora's relationship to her past): Like Rob Ryan, Nora returns to the site of her formative trauma to investigate a current event, and the investigation forces open her repressed memory. Like Rob, her professional competence — reading, cataloging, assessing — is undermined by her personal damage. Like *In the Woods*, the deepest mystery (what exactly happened to Judith in 1981) is not fully solved. Nora will read the journal page, but the reader will not. The story refuses the closure that both Nora and the reader crave. The wound transforms but does not heal.

### 7. Title

**Binding and Foxing**

"Binding" refers to bookbinding (Nora's profession) but also to what binds people together — obligation, guilt, silence, the ties of a closed community. "Foxing" is the brown spotting that appears on old paper — damage that comes from age, moisture, and neglect. It cannot be reversed, only acknowledged. The title captures both Nora's world (antiquarian books) and the story's central concern (the irreversible damage of long-kept secrets). It avoids "The" and echoes none of the existing title structures.

## Notes for the Writer Agent

**Prose voice**: First person, past tense (Nora narrating the night's events with the slight retrospective distance of someone who has come through something). The voice should blend Christie's clarity and precision in the interview scenes with French's atmospheric interiority in the memory scenes and the descriptions of the house. Nora is a bookseller — her metaphors should come from books, paper, binding, typography, marginalia. She catalogs instinctively: she notices the edition of a book on a shelf, the typeface on a letter, the way a page has been cut.

**The space as character**: Crossbell House must be vivid and specific. The writer should establish the geography early — library, kitchen, sitting room, hallway, the hidden passage from pantry to library — because the constraint of space means every movement happens within these rooms. The house's smells (woodsmoke, lavender, damp stone, old paper), sounds (the storm, creaking beams, the clock in the hallway), and textures (worn floorboards, the leather of the wingback chair, the coldness of the stone passage) should be constant presences. The house is not a backdrop — it is a participant.

**What NOT to do**:
- Do not make the interviews purely expository. Each conversation should have its own emotional dynamic — Mrs. Dillard's calm control, Bea's breakdown, Gerald's evasiveness. The information revealed matters, but the WAY it is revealed matters more.
- Do not resolve the mystery of what happened to Judith. The torn journal page is found but not read by the reader. The story is about Nora choosing to face the truth, not about the truth itself.
- Do not make Nora's memory recovery a clean, dramatic flashback. It should be fragmentary, sensory, and uncertain — French's style, not a procedural revelation.
- Do not explain why each guest came to Crossbell House in neat expository paragraphs. Let the reasons leak out through dialogue, through what people say accidentally or defensively.
- Do not use the names "Marcus" or "Chen."

**What TO do**:
- Use the physical constraint of space deliberately. Characters bump into each other in hallways. The kitchen is where alliances form because it is warm and the sitting room is not. The library is where secrets live because it was Philippa's domain. The hidden passage is the architectural secret that mirrors the narrative secret.
- Let the storm be present throughout — audible, pressing against the windows, making the house feel smaller as the night progresses.
- Give each guest a distinctive voice in dialogue — Gerald's legal precision, Mrs. Dillard's Yorkshire practicality, Bea's nervous over-explanation, Rowan's journalist's directness, Dennis's careful silence.
- The cozy mystery element should be genuine — there is pleasure in the detection, warmth in the tea-and-biscuits setting, satisfaction in the unfolding puzzle — even as the darker psychological currents run beneath.
